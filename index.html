Understanding the Curricula

What Are Blue & Green Curricula?

Two UNESCO-backed educational frameworks that bring the ocean and climate change from the margins to the center of learning.

🌊

Blue Curriculum

A framework developed by IOC-UNESCO (2022) that integrates Ocean Literacy into national school curricula. It helps learners understand the ocean's influence on life on Earth — and human influence on the ocean — across all subjects and grade levels.

The essence: "Understanding the ocean's influence on us, and our influence on the ocean." It operates through four pillars: Knowledge & Awareness, Nature-Connectedness, Values & Attitudes, and Competences.

IOC-UNESCO · 2022
🌿

Greening Curriculum

Published by UNESCO in 2024, this guidance helps countries embed climate change and sustainability holistically across all subjects. It targets 90% of all countries to include climate change in curricula by 2030.

It covers six key concepts: Climate Science, Ecosystems & Biodiversity, Climate Justice, Resilience-Building, Post-Carbon Economies, and Sustainable Lifestyles — for ages 5 to 18+.

UNESCO Greening Education Partnership · 2024

Core Concepts

Foundational Principles

🌊 Ocean Literacy: 7 Principles

  • 1
    One OceanThe Earth has one big ocean with many interconnected features.
  • 2
    Ocean Shapes EarthThe ocean and life in it shape the features of our planet.
  • 3
    Climate InfluenceThe ocean has a major influence on weather and climate.
  • 4
    Makes Earth HabitableThe ocean makes Earth livable by producing oxygen and regulating temperature.
  • 5
    Biodiversity HubThe ocean supports enormous diversity of life and ecosystems.
  • 6
    Ocean–Human ConnectionThe ocean and humans are inextricably interconnected.
  • 7
    Largely UnexploredOver 80% of the ocean remains unmapped and unexplored.

🌿 Greening Education: 6 Key Concepts

  • 1
    Climate ScienceWeather, greenhouse gases, the carbon cycle, water cycle, renewable energy, and pollution.
  • 2
    Ecosystems & BiodiversityLand and ocean ecosystems, biodiversity loss, and reconnecting to nature.
  • 3
    Climate JusticeContemporary impacts, social determinants, historical processes, and transformed futures.
  • 4
    Resilience-BuildingCoping with climate impacts, managing climate anxiety, and community action.
  • 5
    Post-Carbon EconomiesCircular economy, clean energy, and reimagining how economies can function sustainably.
  • 6
    Sustainable LifestylesResponsible consumption, mobility, diet, energy use, and waste practices.

Historical Development

How These Curricula Evolved

🌊 Blue Curriculum Timeline

Early 2000s

US educators identified gaps in ocean topics in school curricula; the Ocean Literacy movement began.

2004

Online workshop defined the 7 principles of Ocean Literacy.

2017

IOC-UNESCO embraced Ocean Literacy; "Ocean Literacy for All" toolkit launched. First UN Ocean Conference.

2021–2030

UN Decade of Ocean Science for Sustainable Development declared; Ocean Literacy central to the mission.

2022

"A New Blue Curriculum" Toolkit for Policy-makers published by IOC-UNESCO.

🌿 Greening Curriculum Timeline

1992

UNCED (Earth Summit) in Rio sparked global attention to environmental education.

2015

Adoption of UN Sustainable Development Goals, including SDG 13 (Climate Action) and SDG 4 (Quality Education).

2022

UN Transforming Education Summit calls for climate education across all levels. Greening Education Partnership formed.

2024

UNESCO publishes "Greening Curriculum Guidance: Teaching and Learning for Climate Action" — targeting 90% of countries by 2030.

Real Cases & Issues

Global & Indonesian Context

These curricula respond to urgent real-world crises. Here are key cases from around the world — including Indonesia.

🇵🇹
Blue Curriculum

Portugal – Ocean Integration

Portugal, with its long Atlantic coastline, integrated ocean literacy into national science curricula at all levels, connecting coastal communities with marine science through schools and NGO partnerships.

🇰🇪
Blue Curriculum

Kenya – Coastal Communities

Kenya used local traditional ocean knowledge alongside formal curriculum to engage coastal fishing communities. Students learned about mangrove ecosystems and their role in climate resilience.

🇧🇷
Blue Curriculum

Brazil – Amazon & Ocean

Brazil linked river and ocean ecosystems in school curricula, addressing the impact of deforestation on ocean health and equipping students to advocate for both terrestrial and marine protection.

🇮🇳
Greening Curriculum

India – National Consultation

India's Ministry of Education and National Council of Educational Research participated in UNESCO's greening curriculum pilots, integrating renewable energy and sustainable agriculture into science subjects.

🇿🇼
Greening Curriculum

Zimbabwe – Resilience Focus

Zimbabwe's Ministry of Education integrated climate resilience into primary and secondary curricula, with a focus on how local communities adapt to drought and food insecurity driven by climate change.

🇮🇩
Indonesia

Indonesia – Archipelago Nation at Risk

As the world's largest archipelago with 17,000+ islands, Indonesia faces acute ocean and climate challenges: rising sea levels threaten Jakarta and coastal areas; coral bleaching threatens 51,000 km² of reef; and extreme weather disrupts food systems. Education must address this urgency.

🌏 Global Data Points

The IPCC Sixth Assessment Report (2023) warns that without immediate action, 1 billion people living in coastal areas will face extreme sea-level rise by 2100. Meanwhile, the ocean absorbs 25% of all CO₂ emissions and over 90% of excess heat from global warming — making ocean health inseparable from climate stability. An estimated 600 million people depend on marine fisheries for their livelihoods (FAO, 2022).

Analysis: Indonesian Curriculum

How Does Indonesia Connect?

Analyzing Indonesia's national curriculum policies and science content through the lens of Blue and Green Curriculum principles.

Policy Level Analysis

📋
Kurikulum Merdeka (2022)

Indonesia's latest curriculum framework emphasizes Profil Pelajar Pancasila — one of its six dimensions is Beriman kepada Tuhan dan Berakhlak Mulia which explicitly includes caring for the natural environment (akhlak kepada alam). This aligns directly with Green Curriculum's sustainability ethos.

🌿
Project-Based Learning (P5)

The Projek Penguatan Profil Pelajar Pancasila (P5) requires schools to conduct sustainability-themed projects. Many schools use themes like "Gaya Hidup Berkelanjutan" (Sustainable Lifestyles) and "Kearifan Lokal" (Local Wisdom) — both aligned to Green Curriculum goals.

🏝️
National Marine Policy (KP3K)

Indonesia's national marine spatial planning and fisheries policy recognize the country's dependence on ocean resources. However, ocean literacy has not yet been formally integrated at the curriculum policy level — a gap the Blue Curriculum framework directly addresses.

🎓
RPJMN & SDG Alignment

Indonesia's Medium-Term Development Plan (RPJMN 2020–2024) commits to SDG 4 (Quality Education) and SDG 13 (Climate Action). This creates an official basis for integrating Blue and Green curricula into national education planning.

🌊 Blue Curriculum Connections in Indonesian Science

Topics already present in the Indonesian IPA curriculum that connect to Ocean Literacy:

Ekosistem Laut (SMP) Rantai Makanan Laut Keanekaragaman Hayati Sistem Bumi-Bulan-Matahari Perubahan Iklim (SMA) Pencemaran Lingkungan

Gap identified: While these topics exist, they are taught in fragmented, textbook-based ways without the holistic ocean-systems thinking the Blue Curriculum promotes. Local coastal and maritime cultural knowledge is rarely included.

🌿 Green Curriculum Connections in Indonesian Science

Topics in the Indonesian curriculum that align with Greening Education principles:

Perubahan Iklim Energi Terbarukan Daur Biogeokimia Ekosistem & Suksesi Pengelolaan Sampah Gaya Hidup Sehat

Gap identified: Climate change is addressed factually but lacks the action-oriented, justice-centered, and emotional resilience dimensions that the Greening Curriculum guidance emphasizes. Assessment is predominantly cognitive, not holistic.

SDG Connections in Indonesian Curriculum

Both curricula support Indonesia's commitment to the Sustainable Development Goals:

SDG 4 – Quality Education SDG 13 – Climate Action SDG 14 – Life Below Water SDG 15 – Life on Land SDG 17 – Partnerships

Implementation in Education

How to Implement It

Practical examples of how teachers, students, and citizens can bring these curricula to life.

📚 Sample Lesson: Ocean & Climate Connection

Grade 8 IPA (Junior High) · 2 × 80-minute sessions

  • Students observe local beach/river pollution (or photos if inland) and record findings using a structured guide.
  • Small groups research one ocean ecosystem (coral reef, mangrove, deep sea) and how climate affects it.
  • Create a visual "Ocean Story" connecting local water sources to the global ocean system.
  • Present proposed community actions to address one identified threat and reflect on personal responsibility.

🙋 Who Can Act and How?

Teachers:

Cross-subject ocean projects Field trips to coastal areas Guest: local fishermen or marine researchers Use virtual reality for ocean exploration

Students:

Join ocean clean-up campaigns Reduce single-use plastics Create ocean awareness media (video/poster) Monitor local water quality

Citizens:

Support sustainable seafood choices Participate in coastal ecosystem monitoring

📚 Sample Lesson: Climate Justice & Local Impact

Grade 10 IPA / IPS (Senior High) · 2 × 90-minute sessions

  • Introduce climate data: local temperature trends in Indonesia over 30 years — students identify patterns and anomalies.
  • Discuss who is most affected by climate change in Indonesia (farmers, fishermen, urban poor) — connecting science with social justice.
  • Students design a "Sustainability Action Plan" for their school or neighborhood using the circular economy framework.
  • Conduct a carbon footprint audit of the classroom and propose measurable reduction strategies to present to school leadership.

🙋 Who Can Act and How?

Teachers:

Embed climate in all subjects (Math, Bahasa, IPS) Use place-based pedagogy Facilitate student-led sustainability projects (P5) Invite community leaders & activists

Students:

Start a composting or recycling program at school Plant trees / maintain a school garden Advocate for solar panels in school Join local youth climate movements

Citizens:

Choose renewable energy if available Adopt plant-rich diets Support local environmental policy

Why It Matters

Education Is the Long-Term Solution

Without transforming how we teach, we cannot transform how future generations act on the planet's greatest challenges.

🌍

Systemic Change

Blue and Green curricula go beyond information. They cultivate values, attitudes, and competencies that translate into sustainable behaviors across a lifetime.

⚖️

Justice & Equity

Both frameworks center climate justice — recognizing that those who contribute least to climate change (including island nations like Indonesia) often suffer the most.

🇮🇩

Indonesia's Unique Urgency

As the world's largest archipelago with 17,000+ islands, enormous marine biodiversity, and high climate vulnerability, Indonesia has both the most to lose and the most to gain from these educational transformations.

🔬

21st Century Skills

Ocean and climate literacy build critical thinking, systems thinking, creativity, and global citizenship — exactly the competencies needed for the future workforce.

🤝

Community Connection

Both curricula emphasize connecting schools with local communities, traditional knowledge, and ecosystems — making education relevant, rooted, and culturally meaningful.

🚀

Future Generations

Young people are already demanding climate action. These curricula give them the knowledge and agency to lead the change their generation knows is necessary.
